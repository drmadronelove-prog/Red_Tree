The CIIS Approach to Learning

Experiential. Integral. Socially just. These three commitments shape everything we do in the ICP program — from how we structure a class session to how we understand the therapeutic relationship.

Three Pillars of CIIS Pedagogy

Since its founding in 1968, CIIS has been guided by the conviction that education must transform not only the intellect but the whole person — body, mind, spirit, and community. The ICP program carries this mission forward in the training of future counselors and therapists.

🌊

Experiential Learning

Learning through embodied presence

At CIIS, knowledge is not simply transmitted — it is lived. Experiential learning means that the classroom itself becomes a site of practice: relational, somatic, and reflective.

ICP students are not only learning about therapeutic modalities — they are practicing them with one another, inhabiting the role of both clinician and client in training contexts. This demands that adjunct faculty create brave, boundaried, and generative containers for personal and collective inquiry.

In Practice

  • Opening and closing circles that acknowledge the relational field
  • Embodiment practices, check-ins, and somatic awareness exercises
  • Process-oriented small group work and dyads
  • Regular reflection on the personal impact of clinical material
  • Attention to what is happening in the room as pedagogical data
🌀

Integral Framework

Honoring multiple ways of knowing

The "integral" in CIIS refers to the aspiration to honor multiple dimensions of reality and knowing: psychological, spiritual, somatic, cultural, and systemic. No single theoretical framework captures the full truth of human experience.

Drawn in part from Ken Wilber's Integral Theory and earlier traditions of integral thought (Sri Aurobindo, Jean Gebser), the CIIS approach asks students and teachers alike to hold theory lightly and remain open to complexity, contradiction, and mystery.

In Practice

  • Weaving together psychodynamic, humanistic, somatic, and transpersonal perspectives
  • Valuing indigenous, contemplative, and non-Western epistemologies alongside Western science
  • Inviting students to examine their own theoretical biases and defaults
  • Emphasizing the intersubjective and relational dimensions of healing
  • Engaging with the spiritual and existential dimensions of the therapeutic encounter
✊

Social Justice

Healing in context and community

CIIS has long held that personal healing cannot be fully understood apart from its social and political context. Therapy takes place in a world shaped by racism, colonialism, heteropatriarchy, ableism, and economic injustice — and therapists must be equipped to work skillfully with these realities.

Social justice is not an add-on to the ICP curriculum. It is woven into case conceptualization, theoretical frameworks, clinical supervision, and the classroom culture itself.

In Practice

  • Centering the voices and experiences of marginalized communities in readings and case discussions
  • Ongoing faculty and student reflection on power, privilege, and positionality
  • Critical examination of the history of psychiatry and the DSM
  • Cultural humility as a lifelong practice, not a competency to be achieved
  • Attention to the sociopolitical contexts that shape client presenting concerns

Creating the Learning Container

In ICP, the classroom is not just a place where information is conveyed — it is a relational container within which students practice becoming therapists. This places particular responsibility on adjunct faculty to attend to the quality of the relational field, not just the content.

Safety & Challenge

Effective learning requires enough safety to take risks and enough challenge to grow. ICP faculty attend to this balance actively, rather than assuming it.

The Use of Self

ICP faculty are encouraged to model appropriate self-disclosure, emotional presence, and reflexivity — demonstrating the integration expected of students.

Process Over Product

How the class unfolds — the relational dynamics, the disruptions, the unexpected feelings — is as pedagogically significant as the planned content.

Land Acknowledgment

CIIS is located on unceded Ramaytush Ohlone land. Many ICP faculty open their courses with a land acknowledgment and invite reflection on what this means for healing work.

Community Agreements

Creating explicit community agreements with students at the start of the semester is a common ICP practice. Agreements might cover confidentiality, respect, "oops/ouch," and stepping up/stepping back.

Somatic Attunement

Reading the somatic field of the classroom — noticing collective energy, tension, or aliveness — is a skill ICP faculty develop alongside their students.

Professional Identity Development

A central goal of ICP training is the development of students' professional therapeutic identity — their sense of themselves as ethical, competent, and culturally humble practitioners. Adjunct faculty contribute to this development not only through course content but through how they show up.

The Parallel Process

Just as therapists are shaped by their relationship with supervisors, ICP students are shaped by their relationship with faculty. The quality of how we teach — how we hold discomfort, repair ruptures, acknowledge power, and remain curious — is itself a model of therapeutic presence. This is the parallel process in action.

What Students Are Developing

  • A coherent and flexible theoretical orientation
  • Capacity for empathic attunement across difference
  • Ethical reasoning under complexity and ambiguity
  • Awareness of countertransference and personal triggers
  • Cultural humility and antiracist clinical competence
  • Somatic awareness in the therapeutic relationship
  • Capacity to work with the spiritual and transpersonal

What Faculty Offer

  • Expertise rooted in lived clinical experience
  • Modeling of ongoing self-reflection and learning
  • Honest engagement with the limits of theoretical frameworks
  • Willingness to be moved by student questions and challenges
  • Consistency, clarity, and warmth in the teaching relationship
  • A commitment to the whole student — not just the clinician-in-training
